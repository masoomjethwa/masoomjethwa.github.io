üî≠ What is an Ellipse?

An ellipse is a stretched circle, shaped like an oval.

Unlike a perfect circle that has one center, an ellipse has two centers called foci (singular: focus).

‚ú® Scientific Definition:

An ellipse is a closed curve where the sum of distances from any point on the curve to the two foci is constant.

üåç Why Does It Matter?

All planets, moons, comets, and even spacecraft orbit in elliptical paths ‚Äî not circles.

This is due to the gravitational pull of the Sun or the parent planet, combined with the orbital speed of the body.

ü•á LAW 1: The Law of Ellipses

‚ÄúThe orbit of every planet is an ellipse with the Sun at one of the two foci.‚Äù

  • The Sun is not at the center of the orbit ‚Äî it's off to one side.
  • Planets move closer and farther from the Sun as they orbit.

ü•à LAW 2: The Law of Equal Areas

‚ÄúA line joining a planet and the Sun sweeps out equal areas in equal intervals of time.‚Äù

üîç What it means:

  • Even though a planet moves along a curve, it speeds up when closer to the Sun (at perihelion)
  • And slows down when farther away (at aphelion)
  • Yet, the area it sweeps in space is the same in equal time intervals
Law of Equal Areas

üìå Example:

In one day, the area swept by Earth on January 3 and July 4 is equal ‚Äî though the speed and distance are different.

Term Meaning
T Time to complete one orbit (in Earth years)
