What Are Constellations?

Historically seen as star patterns, the International Astronomical Union (IAU) now defines the 88 constellations as specific, bordered areas of the sky. This system creates a complete map, ensuring every celestial object belongs to one region.

These celestial figures are a mix of animals, objects, and mythological characters, reflecting a rich history of human sky-gazing across cultures.

The Zodiac: Astronomy vs. Astrology

A Tale of Two Zodiacs

Astronomy and astrology view the zodiac differently. Astronomically, the Sun's path (the ecliptic) crosses 13 constellations of varying sizes. Astrology, however, uses a system of 12 equal 30° signs.

This discrepancy includes Ophiuchus, the "13th" zodiac constellation, which is part of the Sun's actual path but is excluded from traditional astrological charts.

The Great Celestial Shift: Axial Precession

Earth's axis wobbles slowly over a ~26,000-year cycle. This "precession" causes the position of the equinoxes to shift against the backdrop of stars. As a result, the astrological signs (fixed to seasons) no longer align with the astronomical constellations they were named after.

~2,000 Years Ago

The March equinox, the starting point for the Tropical Zodiac, was in the constellation Aries. Astrological signs and astronomical constellations were largely aligned.

Present Day

Due to precession, the March equinox has shifted and now occurs when the Sun is in the constellation Pisces. Your astrological sign may not match the constellation the sun was actually in at your birth.

~In 11,800 Years

The cycle will continue. Earth's North Pole will point towards the star Vega instead of Polaris, and the seasons will be reversed relative to our orbit.

Nakshatras: The Lunar Mansions

Vedic astrology uses a system of 27 lunar mansions, or Nakshatras, to provide a finer level of detail than the 12 zodiac signs. Each zodiac sign (Rashi) contains exactly 2.25 Nakshatras, creating a layered system for astrological analysis.

Cosmic Titans: Black Holes

Black holes are regions of spacetime where gravity is so strong that nothing, not even light, can escape. They are not empty space but incredibly dense concentrations of matter. Astronomers classify them by mass, from stellar-mass black holes born from collapsed stars to supermassive giants at the centers of galaxies.

Unlike other celestial phenomena discussed, black holes are a purely scientific concept with no role in traditional astrology.

Our Home: The Milky Way Galaxy

The "Mandakini," or Milky Way, is our home galaxy—a vast, barred spiral system containing billions of stars, including our Sun. It is a complex structure with distinct components, all orbiting a supermassive black hole, Sagittarius A*, at its center.
