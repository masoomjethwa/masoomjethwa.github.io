For centuries, humanity has gazed at the stars and wondered: how did our home, the Solar System, come to be? The Monistic View: A Singular Genesis

The earliest scientific theories proposed that the Sun and planets formed from a single, isolated system. These foundational ideas focused on the power of gravity acting on vast clouds of primordial matter.

üåÄGaseous Hypothesis (1755)

Proposed by: Immanuel Kant

Kant envisioned a giant, slow-spinning cloud of gas. Under its own gravity, this cloud collapsed, with the center igniting to become the Sun and the surrounding material forming concentric rings that would later coalesce into planets.

üîÑNebular Hypothesis (1796)

Proposed by: Pierre-Simon Laplace

Building on Kant's idea, Laplace proposed that the initial cloud was already hot and rotating. As it cooled and contracted, it spun faster, flattening into a disk and shedding successive rings of gas that condensed into planets.

The Dualistic View: A Cosmic Encounter

Later theories introduced a second celestial body, suggesting a dramatic interaction was necessary to pull material from our Sun to form the planets. This marked a shift from isolated formation to dynamic encounters.

Planetesimal Hypothesis (~1905)

Proposed by: Chamberlin & Moulton

In this model, the passing star's gravity didn't just pull out a gas filament; it ripped away solid "planetesimals." These small bodies then gradually clumped together through collisions to build the planets we know today.

Tidal Hypothesis (1919)

Proposed by: James Jeans & Harold Jeffreys

This theory refined the dualistic concept, suggesting the ejected material was a hot, gaseous filament, shaped like a cigar‚Äîthicker in the middle and thinner at the ends. This shape was proposed to explain the size distribution of the planets.

Binary Systems & Beyond

As astronomical observation advanced, theories began to incorporate more complex scenarios, such as the Sun having a stellar companion or planets forming from the vast dust lanes of the galaxy.

Double Star Hypothesis (1939)

By: R.A. Littleton

Posited that the Sun was once part of a binary (two-star) system. A third passing star collided with the Sun's companion, breaking it apart and creating a cloud of debris from which the planets formed.

Interstellar Dust Hypothesis (1943)

By: Otto Schmidt

This theory proposed that the Sun, already formed, captured a vast cloud of interstellar dust and gas as it moved through the galaxy. This captured material then flattened into a disk and formed the planets.

Interstellar Cloud Hypothesis

By: Hannes Alfven

Alfven suggested that electromagnetic forces played a key role. As an interstellar cloud collapsed, it ionized, allowing the Sun's magnetic field to slow the rotation of the core and transfer momentum to the outer disk, aiding planet formation.

While most of these early hypotheses have been superseded, they were crucial in developing our current, widely accepted Solar Nebula Disk Model.
